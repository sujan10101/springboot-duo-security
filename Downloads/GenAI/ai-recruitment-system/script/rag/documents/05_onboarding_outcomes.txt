ONBOARDING OUTCOMES & 90-DAY PERFORMANCE NOTES
===============================================
Source: Engineering Manager Reviews | Period: 2022–2025 | Anonymized

PURPOSE: These notes capture how successfully hired candidates performed in their first 90 days.
They help the AI Review Agent understand which candidate characteristics correlated with strong
performance versus those who struggled, enabling more predictive screening.

---

OUTCOME ON-001 | Role: AI/ML Engineer | Hire Ref: SH-001
---------------------------------------------------------
90-Day Rating: Exceeds Expectations

Performance Summary:
- Shipped first production feature (model serving endpoint) in week 3; ahead of the typical 6-week timeline
- Required minimal hand-holding for code review process and Git workflows
- Proactively identified a data leakage issue in the existing training pipeline that had been causing
  inflated offline metrics — a significant contribution in the first month
- Strong communicator; weekly updates were clear, concise, and technically precise

Skills That Transferred Directly from Hiring Assessment:
- Data intuition (caught leakage in hiring challenge) → caught it in production too
- LangChain/RAG background → immediately contributed to the team's RAG product work
- Comfort with model evaluation → drove improvements to the evaluation harness

Onboarding Challenges:
- Took longer to learn Docker for production use (had only used it locally before); required 2 weeks
  of pairing with the MLOps engineer — this was anticipated and planned for

Key Learning: Candidates who demonstrate strong debugging and data intuition in the interview
(especially in coding challenges with intentional errors or imbalanced data) tend to catch
production issues faster. This is a leading indicator worth weighting heavily.

OUTCOME ON-002 | Role: Backend Engineer | Hire Ref: SH-002
-----------------------------------------------------------
90-Day Rating: Meets Expectations (with strong upward trajectory)

Performance Summary:
- Onboarded to the codebase more slowly than expected; took 5 weeks to make first meaningful commit
- Strong on reliability patterns (circuit breakers, retries) — immediately applied to an existing
  fragile service integration
- GraphQL learning curve was slower than anticipated (had mentioned this as a gap)
- By day 75, was contributing independently and had designed a new service from scratch

Skills That Transferred Directly:
- Kubernetes knowledge was immediately useful — took ownership of a deployment issue in week 2
- Database performance instincts: wrote a complex optimized query that replaced a slow N+1 pattern

Onboarding Challenges:
- GraphQL adoption took ~6 weeks of deliberate study; the gap noted in the interview was real
- Communication style was strong verbally but written documentation was initially sparse;
  addressed through team feedback in week 4

Key Learning: Candidates with Kubernetes experience at the interview level (not just tutorial-level)
contribute on infrastructure issues within weeks. "Tutorial experience" vs "production experience"
distinction is important to probe during interviews. Gaps explicitly acknowledged by candidates
(vs. undisclosed gaps) tend to be addressed more proactively during onboarding.

OUTCOME ON-003 | Role: Frontend Engineer | Hire Ref: SH-003
------------------------------------------------------------
90-Day Rating: Exceeds Expectations

Performance Summary:
- Component library contributions started in week 2 — clear architectural thinking from day one
- Pushed the team to improve accessibility standards; filed 12 accessibility bugs in existing
  components and fixed 8 of them within 90 days
- Next.js was new; learned it in parallel with onboarding and shipped an SSR feature by day 60
- Most impactful early hire in the past 2 years according to engineering manager

Skills That Transferred Directly:
- Component library background → owned library expansion immediately
- Accessibility depth → became the team's de facto accessibility reviewer
- Testing culture → raised team test coverage from 60% to 73% in 90 days

Onboarding Challenges:
- Next.js learning was harder than expected (SSR concepts were new); required structured learning time
- Design system opinions occasionally conflicted with existing patterns; required alignment conversations

Key Learning: Candidates with self-built design systems or component libraries indicate architectural
maturity far above their years of experience. Accessibility knowledge is a strong signal — candidates
who deeply understand accessibility understand their users. Willingness to learn SSR frameworks despite
no experience was accurately predicted by the candidate's rapid adoption of Vite in the interview challenge.

OUTCOME ON-004 | Role: AI/ML Engineer | Hire Ref: SH-004
---------------------------------------------------------
90-Day Rating: Meets Expectations (Below Expectations in weeks 1–4, recovered strongly)

Performance Summary:
- First 30 days were rough — Docker, code review, and collaborative Git workflows (feature branches,
  PRs, CI checks) were all new. The anticipated SWE practices gap was real.
- The mentorship plan was essential; without it, performance would have been below expectations
  through the full 90 days
- By day 45, the SWE practices caught up significantly
- LLM fine-tuning work (the core role requirement) was excellent from day one — immediately
  contributed to the LoRA fine-tuning pipeline
- Research background contributed depth: proposed a better evaluation methodology for fine-tuned models
  that the team adopted company-wide

Skills That Transferred Directly:
- LLM fine-tuning → exactly what was needed; significant immediate impact
- ML fundamentals depth → improved evaluation methodology
- Research rigor → better experimental design

Onboarding Challenges:
- SWE practices gap was significant (as predicted); mentorship was necessary, not optional
- Needed structured time to learn Git branching strategy, code review, and CI/CD

Key Learning: Research-to-industry transitions require explicit mentorship on SWE practices.
When the hiring panel notes "SWE practices gap," a mentorship plan must be created before day one —
not after problems emerge. The core technical depth (in this case, fine-tuning) transfers reliably;
the peripheral SWE practices can be taught with the right support.

OUTCOME ON-005 | Role: Backend Engineer | Hire Ref: SH-005
-----------------------------------------------------------
90-Day Rating: Exceeds Expectations

Performance Summary:
- Self-taught background created no observable gaps in first 90 days
- Open-source experience meant strong code quality and PR etiquette from day one
- Kubernetes was new; learned it in weeks 1–3 through documentation and team pairing; contributing
  by week 4 — faster than most candidates with claimed Kubernetes experience
- Multi-tenant SaaS architecture contributions started in week 6; designed a tenant isolation pattern
  improvement that is now the team standard

Skills That Transferred Directly:
- Open-source code quality standards → immediately production-ready code
- Multi-tenant thinking → direct architectural contributions
- Billing/payments background → surprised the team with domain knowledge relevant to a new feature

Onboarding Challenges:
- CS fundamentals gaps (algorithms, formal data structures) surfaced in one whiteboard session;
  did not affect practical work but required some conceptual grounding
- Kubernetes onboarding took focused effort (as anticipated)

Key Learning: Open-source maintainers tend to have strong code quality, PR etiquette, and documentation
habits that are harder to teach than technical skills. Self-taught candidates with production deployments
and real users often have stronger practical instincts than academically credentialed candidates
without production experience. The gap should be verified — not assumed.

PREDICTIVE SIGNALS SUMMARY (Based on 90-Day Outcomes):
=======================================================

STRONG POSITIVE PREDICTORS:
1. Caught/identified bugs or data issues in the interview challenge → predicts production bug-finding
2. Open-source projects with real users → predicts code quality and documentation habits
3. Self-built tools or libraries → predicts architectural thinking and initiative
4. Explicitly acknowledged gaps (rather than hiding them) → predicts self-aware, coachable behavior
5. Rapid technology adoption demonstrated during interview (e.g., picked up new tool mid-challenge) → predicts onboarding speed
6. Research publications → predicts rigor and depth in ML roles specifically

MODERATE POSITIVE PREDICTORS:
7. Side projects with real deployments (not just local/demo) → predicts deployment readiness
8. Strong testing culture → predicts production code quality
9. Accessibility knowledge in frontend candidates → predicts user empathy and holistic thinking

RISK FACTORS TO WATCH:
10. Gaps between claimed skill level and demonstrated level → requires specific probing question
11. SWE practices gap in research candidates → plan mentorship before day one
12. New technology adoption required for role + no evidence of rapid learning → high onboarding risk
13. Limited production deployment experience → pair with experienced engineer immediately on day one
